Outcome One:

Evaluate the organizational structure of a business in order to facilitate data management.


Artifact 1: "Agile" Activity Diagram

This “Activity Diagram” shows the process and hierarchy of the flow of how a development company might handle and process a customer, or client, request. Each column represents a different branch of the company and shows the flow of responsibility from one department to another and how they relate to each other.

As you can see, a client submits a request for a project. Initially, the design team crafts a rough idea of how the project should look. If the “order” is rejected by the client, the Project Design Team starts the process over until the offer is “accepted”. The project is then sent to the Front Side Development Team and the Database/Backside Development Team. The two teams then work together to ensure their structures and ideas are merge-able.

After a final product is developed, it is sent to a Maintenance group that would do the beta/bug testing. The product is then shipped out to the customer where they would find the appropriate avenue for distribution, be it physically or digitally.


Artifact 2: "Agile" Use Case Diagram

This is a “Use Case Diagram” created for a faux company Called Indicia Data (ID). ID is a Data Solutions company that gets hired by businesses to manage all of their digital data.

The diagram shows a user or a client hiring ID to backup and manage their data. They gain access to an ID controlled web portal allowing the client to have rough overview access to the process that ID will be working on for this client. ID also uses this web portal to communicate with the client.

The web portal syncs with the remote database set up for the client, which ID also manages. ID then builds on site backups for all of the client’s data where backups are made for redundancy and in turn, syncs back to the remote database. The web portal continues to act as “home base” for communications between ID and the client.


Outcome 1 Reflection

These examples were taken from my Business Architecture class which focused on the structure and process of what a business goes through to ensure customer satisfaction in relation to a Database Company. These activities and projects were an excellent way to visualize and conceptualize how this process would work in a real world scenario.

I learned how many steps and inner-processes it takes to implement a fully functional, working, and effective business model of the workflow that a company would need to implement to ensure that a client’s needs were met. Each client needs their own specialized plan for their specific needs. The diagrams not only help the company with their workflow, but also can be presented to the client to help articulate the process that goes on behind the scenes. This keeps the client “in the loop” and gives them piece of mind that the company they have hired has a plan and an end goal for their project.

Every team, including each member of that team, has a specific job and “order of operations” to ensure the project gets developed in a timely manner while keeping the client in the loop. This process taught me that each team member is a necessary cog in the machine to bring an idea to reality. Work is divided among team members who all communicate with each other and ultimately release a suitable product for the client. Each step can be reviewed and edited as many times as is necessary to satisfy the client and their needs. A client can be an individual or a company.

Outcome Three:

Determine that a Database is Properly Structured.


Artifact 1: Data Normalization

Often times when dealing with raw data, you are given flat file documents like Excel spreadsheets or other forms of poorly organized data. The job of a good database designer is to take these documents and perform what is called Data Normalization. There are several steps to taking the data you see below and turning into a visual representation of what tables and columns you will need when it comes to constructing the database.

Through the normalization process, I learned how to identify redundant data, ensure that all data had a proper home, and how to identify the key fields of what would become the database. This is a good example of how a company can come to you with a set of raw, unorganized data with the desire to transform it into a Relational Database and the steps you would take to make that happen for them.

With normalization, I transformed the above data set into a fully normalized visual representation of what the database would look like. An example of that can be found below. All of the data redundancies have been removed and the future database tables have been named and ordered. This process makes it simple to take this data, enter it in a database, and ensure that it is properly structured.


Artifact 2: Database Planning: Initial Layout Sketch

This rough sketch of a database was used for my group’s final capstone project for an online web application called a “Student Response System”. The sketch shows the tables that will be implemented into the database, along with the columns in each table, and the data types associated with each field/column. Stars next to certain columns indicate that that column as a “Primary Key” in the table. “FK” next to a column indicates that column is used as a “Foreign Key” in that table, which is a primary key on another table. This is the basis of a Relational Database Management System.

Outcome 3 Reflection

The process of structuring a database can be a tedious, yet vital step in getting a database up and running for a company or website. The process starts with normalization and ends with the actual implementation of the data and ideas built during the planning stage into a live, functional relational database.

Through this multi-step process, I learned how data within the tables relates to each other, and how important it is to set up the appropriate indexes and key restraints within the database. The true power in a RDBMS comes from joining fields together so that you can create unique and meaningful queries between all the sets of data. I’ve learned that a strong, properly structured database is essential to any company or web service operating in an efficient way. Online stores, data driven websites, and even WordPress installations all depend on these databases to function and serve their customers.

Outcome Five:

Articulate development solutions to peers and supervisors.


Artifact 1: Agile Development and User Stories

Agile development is a design philosophy that involves a multi-step repeatable process of gathering input from potential users of your product or service, preparing a backlog of tasks to accomplish, and setting small, achievable goals that you present on a regular basis called “sprints”. This process allows the development team to always have a ‘working’ product to show the client after each sprint phase.

In my capstone project, my team has been using agile development to develop our web application.

In Figure 1, you can see an example of a user story. This is the first step in the agile process. It involves collecting a list of “requirements” and desired outcomes for each user of your product. They are collected in story format to ensure the developers can accomplish not only a specific task, but achieve the overall desire of what a user is looking for from their product.


Artifact 2: Agile Development and Feature Backlog

A feature backlog is a list of features that the client and developers want to implement in their product. This is where you make note of features that you wish to build and also a place you can make not of potential pitfalls and ask questions about what features are necessary or optional. It sets the pace for the development cycle and works as an ongoing “to do” list.

Figure 2 lists a backlog we have used for one portion of our project. It lists the features we wish to implement and is an ongoing process of checking with the client and editing the requirements that need to be built into the project. Every aspect of the project can have a backlog, and an overarching backlog is formed in the end to ensure all features have been implemented.


Outcome 5 Reflection

The process of agile development has taught me how to not only plan a project, but how to gather relevant information about the audience that will be using the product. It is difficult to get out of your own “developer” thought process while building an application, and this style of development allows you to connect with your client in unique ways and to constantly keep them up to date with the latest iterations. This allows for constant feedback from your client, enabling you to explain and articulate the development goals in a way that the client can understand because they always have a working copy of each iteration after each sprint is concluded. It eliminates the amount of “developer speak” that takes place between developer and client.

I have learned that the process of agile development keeps you close to your client while ultimately achieving your goals as a developer.
